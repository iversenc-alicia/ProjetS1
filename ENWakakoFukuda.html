Wakako Fukuda, the feminism’s voice among a heavy silence

Wakako Fukuda is not known in the way that other feminist figures are known. However, every feminist who acts is important. Indeed, it is by daring to call her/himself a feminist that more people will in turn become one. Equality between men and women will then become evidence. However, Wakako is still the founder of a feminist movement that is adapted to Japan. She is one of the extreme minority of Japanese women who dare to protest for their identity. But in a country where women are supposed to be silent,standing up is far from being easy.

Founder of a feminist movement adapted to Japan

During her trip to Germany, she became aware of the #MeToo movement. When she came back to Japan in 2018, she founded the feminist movement called "I am".It is a feminist movement that takes the the very particular context of Japan. into consideration. It is important to understand that in Japan many women are not aware of being treated badly. The name of the movement is close to #MeToo.However, it is more focused on the individual and encourages women to have the courage to speak up for themselves.

Then, the fact that Japan normalizes many things that are considered unacceptable today in the Occident must be taken into. Many examples are citables. The many women who are followed daily to their homeson a much bigger scale than in France because the Japanese men are often too "shy" to speak to them and prefer to follow them. There are alsometro cars and stairways only for women because there are too many sexual abuses. So it is essential in Japan that women make their own voices heard, but for many, they stay silent. That is why, among this silence, Wakako's voice echoes so much and makes her a crucial figure for Japan.

Many people do not consider a woman who goes out to protest for her rights as a feminist figure. But in Japan, in the middle of all this silence, raising your voice is far from easy. Women like Wakako Fukuda are determining forces.

Her complicated beginning in feminism

In a country like Japan, where we are not expecting a woman to express her opinions, there is a Japanese woman who does not hesitate to say out loud what she thinks. Her name is Wakako Fukuda. Japanese women are supposed to be quiet and respectful. It is in this context that Wakako evolves. She does not hesitate to lead big crowds of protestors and make speeches where she says things like "I'd like to feel free to give life to a girl into a society that would be safe for her, in a country where she could express herself without any fear. During her first manifestations she received threats like "you deserve to be raped".hreats that show the hostile environment that women have to deal with every day.

Childhood

Wakako Fukudawas raised in a classic family pattern. Her father is a journalist and her mother is a housewife. This is still a very common pattern in Japan compared to other developed countries. When she was young she would sometimes skip school to read books, especially books on feminism. She has read a lot of books by two famous Japanese feminists: Noe Itô and Eimi Yamata. When she was a high school student she read about the law that was threatening the freedom of expression in Japan. Revolted, it was at that time that she started to go out into the streets to make her voice heard.
So it is pretty rare to see women like Wakako in Japan. Aware of her identity, she did not hesitate to inform herself when she was younger. Then she grew up affirming her demands in the middle of a heavy silence.

Feminists that inspired Wakako Fukuda

Eimi Yamata

Eimi Yamada is a writer and a mangaka born in 1959. She studied literature at Meiji University. Yamada's works appeared for the first time in the beginning of the modern period. Her first book was published in 1985 and it is called Bitter Voluptuousness. It is an autobiographical work in which she tells about one of her sexual passions. In a society that is mostly male-centred, Eimi Yamada is one of the first authors to write for women. Her writing can be seen as a sexual liberation for women. She published other novels showing a desire to show her sexual freedom, like in 1988 with "Kneel down and lick my feet".

Noe Itô

Noe Ito is from a completely different time but she is still a figure in Japanese feminism. She was born in 1895 in Imakuju and she died in 1923 in Fukuoka. In her 28 years of life, she did not miss to mark history. She was one of the women who participated in the feminist revue Seito. She started a line about the problems that women faced in Japan's society at this time. In this, she talked about abortion, forced marriages and prostitution. Later, she became an advisor for the feminist and socialist organisation Sekirankai.

Other Japanese women who campaigned for the improvement of women's status

Raicho Hiratsuka

Raichô Hiratsuka was a Japanese feminist writer, journalist and politician born in 1886 and she died in 1971. She created the first feminist revue Seitô in 1911. This revue hosted many Japanese feminist militants, as we will mention below.

Kishida Toshiko

KKishida Toshiko was born in 1864 and passed away in 1901. She was a calligrapher and teacher in the Imperial Palace. She was one of the few women of that period to be educated. She was also a feminist. Her pseudonym as a writer was Shoen and she is seen as one of the first pioneers of Japanese feminism. At the age of 20 she wrote her first feminist text, The Path of Women.

Fukuda Hideko

Fukuda Hideko was born in 1865 and died in 1927 at the age of 61. She was a feminist teacher of Meiji era who was strongly inspired by Kishida Toshiko. She was heavily influenced by Meiji's restoration and the changes that came with it. She fought for the status of women during her whole life and opened schools for women's education. During a trial where she was judged for the stealing of weapons and military equipment, she was called " the Japanese Jeanne d'Arc ".

Waka Yamada

Waka Yamada was born in 1879 and passed away in 1957. During her youth she was kidnapped and sold as a sexual slave in the USA. She managed to escape and return to Japan where she also contributed to the feminist revue Seito. After surviving hell, she established the Mothers' Protection League in 1934. In 1938, she built Japan's first refuge for abused women and children.

Fusae Ichikawa

Fusae Ichikawa was a politician and feminist who was born in 1893 and who died in 1981. She was revolted by the three submission rule for women and went into politics. She helped to win the right to vote for women in Japan. She founded the first organisation dedicated to it.

Glossary

#MeToo:
#MeToo is a solidarity movement allowing women to speak out about sexual assault, harassment and sexism in their daily lives. The purpose is for women to see that they are not alone and to speak out in their turn. The French version is known as #BalanceTonPorc.
